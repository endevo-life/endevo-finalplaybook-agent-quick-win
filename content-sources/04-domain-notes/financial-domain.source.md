# Financial domain — deterministic questions (source)

Real content from Niki/Andrea's model. Each question has tiered answer options;
each answer maps to an intro sentence + either a **review checklist** (when
already done) or numbered **steps** (when partial/not done). This is the
canonical deterministic pattern for the whole app.

Legend: `result_type: review` = give a checklist to confirm; `result_type: steps`
= give ordered steps to complete.

## F1 — Financial access & documents

### F1.1 If you died or became incapacitated tomorrow, would the person managing your money know exactly which financial institutions to call and who to ask for?

- **Yes, they have the names and contacts in writing** → *review*
  - Intro: Review your financial contact list to make sure it still reflects your current accounts and institutions. Financial details change often — confirming everything is accurate ensures your agent can act quickly without confusion or delays.
  - Checklist:
    - Are all institutions still correct and active?
    - Have you opened or closed any accounts recently?
    - Are the contact names and phone numbers still accurate?
    - Does your agent know where this list is stored?
- **They know some institutions but not all the contacts** → *steps*
  - Intro: Complete your financial contact list so your agent has everything they need in an emergency. Partial information can cause delays or missed accounts — giving them a full list ensures your finances are handled smoothly and correctly.
  - Steps:
    1. Gather all your financial institutions (banks, credit unions, investment accounts, retirement accounts, insurance companies). Agents need a complete picture.
    2. Add contact names, phone numbers, and departments for each institution. Clear contacts help your agent reach the right person quickly.
    3. Update the list with any new accounts or changes. Keeping it current prevents confusion later.
    4. Give your agent a copy and tell them where the original is stored. They may need it urgently in an emergency.
- **No** → *steps*
  - Intro: Create a complete list of your financial institutions and contacts so your agent can manage your money without delays. Not having this information is extremely common — but it means your agent may struggle to locate accounts or reach the right people when it matters most.
  - Steps:
    1. Write down every financial institution you use (banks, investments, retirement accounts, insurance). This gives your agent a clear starting point.
    2. Add contact names, phone numbers, and departments. This helps your agent get help quickly.
    3. Store the list somewhere secure and accessible. Your agent needs to know exactly where it is.
    4. Give your agent a copy. Having it in their hands ensures they can act immediately if needed.

### F1.2 Do you have a written list of every recurring subscription and autopay account you have?

- **Yes, complete list** → *review*
  - Intro: Review your subscription and autopay list to make sure it still reflects everything you're currently paying for. These accounts change often — confirming your list is accurate helps prevent missed payments, surprise charges, or forgotten subscriptions.
  - Checklist:
    - Have you added any new subscriptions recently?
    - Have you canceled anything that should be removed?
    - Are all payment methods still correct?
    - Does your financial agent know where this list is stored?
- **Partial list** → *steps*
  - Intro: Complete your subscription and autopay list so your financial agent can manage your accounts without confusion. Partial information can lead to missed payments or overlooked charges — a full list ensures everything is handled smoothly.
  - Steps:
    1. Review your bank and credit card statements for recurring charges. This helps you identify subscriptions you may have forgotten.
    2. Add every subscription and autopay account to your list, including streaming services, apps, memberships, insurance, and utilities. A complete list prevents surprises.
    3. Include payment methods and renewal dates. Clear details help your agent manage or cancel accounts if needed.
    4. Store the list somewhere secure and tell your agent where it is. They may need it quickly in an emergency.
- **No** → *steps*
  - Intro: Create a complete list of your subscriptions and autopay accounts so your financial agent can manage your money without delays or missed payments. Not having this list is extremely common — but it can cause financial confusion if someone needs to step in.
  - Steps:
    1. Check your bank and credit card statements for recurring charges. This reveals all active subscriptions.
    2. Write down each subscription or autopay account, including the service name and payment method.
    3. Add renewal dates or billing cycles. This helps your agent stay ahead of upcoming charges.
    4. Store the list somewhere secure and accessible.
    5. Give your financial agent a copy so they can act quickly if needed.

### F1.3 If you died or were incapacitated tomorrow, could your trusted person locate your housing documents (your deed if you own, your lease if you rent)?

- **Yes, it is in writing** → *review*
  - Intro: Review your housing document instructions to make sure they still reflect your current home and storage location. Housing documents are essential for legal and financial processes — confirming everything is accurate ensures your trusted person can act quickly without confusion.
  - Checklist:
    - Is the deed or lease still stored where you said it is?
    - Have you moved or changed residences since writing this?
    - Does your trusted person still know how to access the documents?
    - Have you added any new housing-related paperwork they should know about?
- **I have picked someone but have not made it official** → *steps*
  - Intro: Make your housing document instructions clear and accessible so your trusted person can find what they need immediately. Verbal choices or assumptions can lead to delays — written instructions ensure they know exactly where to look.
  - Steps:
    1. Write down where your deed or lease is stored. Clear directions prevent confusion during stressful moments.
    2. Confirm your trusted person knows they are responsible for this. A simple conversation helps them feel prepared.
    3. Store your housing documents in a secure, consistent location. Executors and agents need quick access.
    4. Give your trusted person a copy of the instructions or tell them exactly where to find them. This ensures they can act without hesitation.
- **No** → *steps*
  - Intro: Create clear written instructions so your trusted person can locate your deed or lease without delays. Not having this information is extremely common — but it can cause major complications if someone needs to manage your housing quickly.
  - Steps:
    1. Identify where your deed or lease is currently stored. Executors and agents need this information immediately in emergencies.
    2. Write down the exact location and any access details. Specific instructions prevent confusion.
    3. Store the document somewhere secure and easy to reach. Housing documents should be protected but accessible.
    4. Tell your trusted person where the document is and how to access it. They may need it urgently if something happens.

### F1.4 If you died or were incapacitated tomorrow, could your trusted person locate the titles to every vehicle you own (cars, motorcycles, RVs, boats, trailers, recreational vehicles)?

- **Yes, all titles are stored securely and they know where** → *review*
  - Intro: Review your vehicle title storage to make sure everything is still secure, accessible, and clearly organized. Vehicle titles are essential for transferring ownership — confirming your trusted person can find them quickly prevents delays or complications.
  - Checklist:
    - Are all titles still stored in the same secure location?
    - Does your trusted person still know how to access them?
    - Have you bought or sold any vehicles recently?
    - Are digital copies backed up somewhere safe?
- **Some titles are stored, some are not, or they are scattered** → *steps*
  - Intro: Consolidate all your vehicle titles into one secure, accessible place so your trusted person can find them without confusion. Scattered or missing titles can cause major delays — keeping them together ensures smooth handling if something happens.
  - Steps:
    1. Gather all vehicle titles from each location. Executors and agents need everything in one place to avoid confusion.
    2. Identify any missing titles and request replacements from your state's DMV. Having complete documentation prevents legal delays.
    3. Choose a single fireproof/waterproof storage solution. This protects your titles and simplifies access.
    4. Move all titles into the consolidated location. Consistency helps your trusted person know exactly where to look.
    5. Tell your trusted person where the storage is and how to access it. Clear communication now prevents problems later.
- **No** → *steps*
  - Intro: Store all your vehicle titles in one secure, fireproof/waterproof location and make sure your trusted person knows where they are. Not having a central storage spot is extremely common — but it can cause major delays when someone needs to transfer or manage your vehicles.
  - Steps:
    1. Locate each vehicle title (cars, motorcycles, RVs, boats, trailers, recreational vehicles). Executors need complete access.
    2. Request replacements for any missing titles through your state's DMV. This ensures everything is legally documented.
    3. Choose a fireproof/waterproof storage solution. Titles must be protected from damage.
    4. Place all titles together in the same location. A single storage spot prevents confusion.
    5. Tell your trusted person where the storage is and how to access it. They may need it urgently in an emergency.

## F2 — Insurance, tax & executor

### F2.1 Have you reviewed your life insurance coverage in the last 2 years to make sure it still matches your obligations (dependents, mortgage, debts, business)?

- **Yes, they know about every policy and where to find them** → *review*
  - Checklist: dependents/beneficiaries changed? mortgage/debt changed? new obligations (business, loans, childcare)? trusted person knows where each policy is stored?
- **They know about some policies but not all** → *steps*
  1. Gather all your life insurance policies (employer-provided, private, term, whole life). People often forget older or workplace policies.
  2. Create a simple list of each policy, including company name, policy number, and contact information.
  3. Review your coverage to make sure it still matches your current obligations.
  4. Give your trusted person copies and tell them where originals are stored.
- **No / Not applicable** → *steps*
  1. Consider your current obligations (dependents, mortgage, debts, business responsibilities).
  2. Decide whether life insurance fits your situation.
  3. If needed, explore term life insurance — simple, affordable, income replacement.
  4. Store any new policy documents securely and tell your trusted person where they are.

### F2.2 If you died tomorrow, could your trusted person locate every life insurance policy you have (personal, employer-provided, mortgage, accidental death) and know which carrier to file the claim with?

- **Yes, reviewed and current** → *review*
  - Checklist: all policies active? coverage added/removed recently? carrier names/contacts correct? trusted person knows where each policy is stored?
- **I have coverage but have not reviewed it recently** → *steps*
  1. Gather all policies (personal, employer-provided, mortgage-related, accidental death).
  2. Create a list with each carrier's name, policy number, and claim contact.
  3. Check coverage still matches current obligations.
  4. Give trusted person copies and tell them where originals are stored.
- **No coverage or never reviewed** → *steps*
  1. Review current obligations to determine if coverage is needed.
  2. If it makes sense, consider term life insurance.
  3. Store any new policy documents securely and tell your trusted person.
  4. Create a simple list of carriers and contacts so claims can be filed quickly.

### F2.3 Have you talked with an accountant or tax professional about the federal and state estate taxes (and any state inheritance taxes) your heirs could face?

- **Yes, reviewed in the last 2 years** → *review*
  - Checklist: assets changed significantly? federal/state exemption amounts changed? now live in a state with inheritance tax? tax professional has current documents?
- **We have talked about it generally but not recently or in detail** → *steps*
  1. Gather your current asset list (home, accounts, investments, business interests).
  2. Schedule a review with an accountant or estate-tax specialist.
  3. Ask about both federal estate tax and your state's estate/inheritance tax rules.
  4. Update your estate plan based on their recommendations.
- **No** → *steps*
  1. Review your total assets to see whether federal estate tax could apply.
  2. Check your state's rules — some have estate taxes, inheritance taxes, or both.
  3. If needed, talk with a tax professional.
  4. Update your estate plan to reflect any tax considerations.

### F2.4 Does your Executor know they will need to open an estate bank account and file a final tax return for you (and possibly an estate tax return) before your assets can be distributed?

- **Yes, we have talked through both responsibilities** → *review*
  - Checklist: know which bank to use for the estate account? know where tax documents are stored? financial accounts changed recently? know who your accountant/tax preparer is?
- **They know one piece but not both** → *steps*
  1. Explain that an estate bank account is required to collect funds, pay bills, distribute assets (executors cannot use personal accounts).
  2. Tell them they must file the final tax return — and possibly an estate tax return.
  3. Show them where tax documents and financial records are stored.
  4. Provide contact info for your accountant/tax preparer.
- **No** → *steps*
  1. Explain the estate bank account requirement.
  2. Tell them about the final + possible estate tax return, required before distribution.
  3. Gather tax documents and store them securely.
  4. Provide accountant/tax preparer contact info.
  5. Write down these instructions so your executor can reference them.

## F3 — Long-term care funding & survivor benefits

### F3.1 If you became incapacitated tomorrow and needed long-term care (nursing home, in-home care, memory care), do you know exactly how it would be paid for?

- **Yes, I have a written plan (LTC insurance, savings, Medicaid strategy, family agreement)** → *review*
  - Checklist: LTC insurance active/adequate? savings/financial situation changed? Medicaid strategy still applies? trusted person knows where plan is stored?
- **I have a general idea but nothing documented** → *steps*
  1. Identify your likely care options (nursing home, in-home care, memory care).
  2. Review your financial resources (insurance, savings, income, family support).
  3. Write down how each type of care would be paid for.
  4. Store the plan securely and tell your trusted person where it is.
- **No** → *steps*
  1. Learn typical costs of nursing homes, in-home care, memory care in your area.
  2. Review your financial resources.
  3. Consider whether LTC insurance or a Medicaid strategy makes sense.
  4. Write down your plan and store it securely.
  5. Tell your trusted person where the plan is.

### F3.2 Do your spouse, dependents, or beneficiaries know what survivor benefits they are entitled to from your employer, pension, retirement plan, and Social Security — and how to actually claim them?

- **Yes, documented and they know how to claim** → *review*
  - Checklist: employer/pension benefits active? changed jobs/retirement plans recently? Social Security survivor benefits explained? beneficiaries know where documents are stored?
- **They know some benefits exist but not all or how to claim** → *steps*
  1. Gather all employer-provided benefits (life insurance, pension, retirement plan, survivor benefits).
  2. Create a list of each benefit — company name, plan type, claim contact.
  3. Add Social Security survivor benefit info — what they qualify for and how to apply.
  4. Give beneficiaries copies and tell them where originals are stored.
- **No** → *steps*
  1. Identify all employer-provided benefits.
  2. Add Social Security survivor benefit info — most families qualify for something.
  3. Write down how each benefit is claimed (phone, website, required documents).
  4. Store the list securely and tell beneficiaries where it is.
  5. Update the list whenever your job or benefits change.
